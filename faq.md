# RESPONSES:

## Teaching & Learning

### How do you engage reluctant learners?

I usually start by looking at the design of the learning experience before I make assumptions about motivation. A learner who appears reluctant may actually be unsure where to start, unclear on the purpose of the task, overloaded by the instructions, or carrying a history of not feeling successful in that kind of work.

My strongest work has been in creating hybrid learning environments where the core structure is embedded into the resource or platform, and my role becomes more like tutoring, facilitation, conferencing, and point-of-need support. That can work well for reluctant learners because they are not waiting passively for the next instruction. They have a clear pathway, visible expectations, and enough flexibility to move at an appropriate pace.

In VCE VM Literacy modules such as *Voice* and *Persuasion*, I have used this kind of structure to combine asynchronous delivery with direct support. Students can access the task sequence, models, prompts, and assessment expectations through the learning materials, while I work alongside them to clarify, extend, redirect, or conference around their progress.

I also like to use inquiry as a way into learning. In Digital Technologies, for example, exploring the history of technology can open up branching discussions about communication, tools, systems, games, and programming. With younger students, that can become a pathway into branching narratives, early computational thinking, and programming concepts without beginning from a dry technical definition.

### What does effective teaching look like in your classroom?

Effective teaching, for me, looks like a well-designed learning environment supported by responsive human facilitation. I want students to know what they are doing, why they are doing it, what success looks like, and where they can go next.

In practice, that means I often build the sequence into the learning materials themselves. The resource does part of the teaching: it stages the task, provides examples, prompts reflection, and makes expectations visible. Then I can spend more time working with students individually or in small groups, rather than constantly holding the whole class at the same point.

That is also where my teaching experience connects strongly with learning design. I am interested in how the structure of a module, workbook, slide deck, form, spreadsheet, or LMS workflow can reduce friction for the learner and create more space for meaningful support.

The best learning environments are not just well-delivered; they are well-designed. They make the next step visible.

### How do you balance explicit instruction with student agency?

I see explicit instruction and student agency as complementary. Students make better choices when the learning pathway is clear.

My usual approach is to define the structure first: the purpose, the model, the criteria, and the steps. Once that structure is visible, students can make meaningful choices within it. That choice might be about topic, format, pathway, tool, level of challenge, or how they demonstrate understanding.

This is especially important in applied learning. Students need enough scaffolding to avoid being lost, but enough ownership to feel that the work belongs to them. In the *Voice* and *Persuasion* modules, for example, the learning sequence can be structured while still allowing students to develop responses that connect to their own interests, opinions, and contexts.

For me, agency does not mean removing structure. It means designing the structure so students can operate within it with increasing independence.

### How do you measure learning growth?

I measure learning growth by looking at progression over time, not just the final product. A single submitted task rarely tells the whole story.

I use checkpoints, conferencing, rubrics, draft feedback, learner reflection, and progress tracking to understand where a student is starting from, what they are beginning to control, and what support they need next. I am particularly interested in systems that make growth visible to the learner, not just to the teacher.

That is where spreadsheets have become an important part of my practice. A well-designed spreadsheet can become more than an administrative tool. It can clarify progress, reveal patterns, support moderation, and help students understand where they are in the learning sequence.

In learning design terms, I am interested in evidence. What does the learner need to show? When will they show it? How will feedback move them forward? How can the system make that process clearer for everyone involved?

### What role does technology play in your teaching?

Technology should reduce friction, increase clarity, or create learning opportunities that would otherwise be difficult to manage. If it does not serve the learning, I do not think it needs to be there.

In my practice, technology often supports hybrid delivery. I use digital platforms, structured documents, forms, slides, and spreadsheets to create learning pathways that students can move through with a degree of independence. That allows classroom time to become more focused on tutoring, feedback, facilitation, and discussion.

I tend to think “open first, closed when needed.” Markdown, spreadsheets, HTML, and simple structured systems are central to how I work because they are flexible, portable, and efficient. More specialised tools are useful when the task calls for them, but I do not want the tool to become the centre of the learning design.

The goal is not to use more technology. The goal is to design better systems for learning.

##  Learning Design

### What is your learning design process?

My learning design process usually starts with the outcome and the learner. I want to know what the learner needs to understand, what they need to be able to do, and what evidence would show that the learning has been successful.

From there, I work backwards. I map the assessment evidence, the key concepts, the likely barriers, the examples learners will need, and the sequence of practice. I then build the learning pathway in a way that makes the process visible: what to do, how to begin, what quality looks like, and how to improve.

I draw on frameworks such as Cognitive Load Theory, UDL, Gagné, ADDIE, and Action Mapping, but I try not to treat them as labels. For me, the practical question is always: does this design reduce confusion, support access, build capability, and lead to meaningful evidence of learning?

A lot of my process happens through simple tools: markdown for structure, spreadsheets for systems and tracking, and then other platforms as needed. I like tools that let me think clearly before I make the design more polished.

### How do you approach curriculum development?

I approach curriculum development as a translation task. The formal curriculum sets the requirements, but learners need those requirements turned into clear, practical, assessable learning experiences.

In VCE VM Work Related Skills and Numeracy, this means taking broad outcomes and designing tasks that feel authentic rather than artificial. Learners need to see how the curriculum connects to work, community, personal decision-making, communication, problem-solving, and the kinds of evidence they can actually produce.

With module development, my strongest examples sit in Literacy and Work Related Skills. I like designing sequences where students can move through structured learning materials, respond to prompts, build evidence over time, and receive feedback without the whole process depending on one live explanation from the teacher.

Good curriculum design should make the pathway clearer for both the learner and the facilitator. It should answer: What are we building toward? What does evidence look like? What support is available? What can be done independently, and where does human facilitation add the most value?

### How do you design for diverse learners?

I design for diverse learners by building flexibility and clarity into the learning experience from the beginning. Adjustment should not feel like an afterthought.

In practical terms, that means clear instructions, visible sequencing, worked examples, scaffolded responses, multiple ways into the task, and options for how learners demonstrate understanding where appropriate. I also try to design tasks with layered success, so that all learners can meet the core requirement while more confident learners can extend.

This is where Universal Design for Learning has influenced my practice, but I tend to think about it in very practical terms. Can the learner understand the task? Can they start? Can they see what success looks like? Can they access support without losing ownership of the work?

For me, inclusive design is not about lowering expectations. It is about removing unnecessary barriers so that the actual learning can become the challenge.

### What learning design models influence your work?

I use learning design models as thinking tools rather than as a script.

Cognitive Load Theory helps me think about clarity, sequencing, and not overwhelming learners with unnecessary complexity. Universal Design for Learning helps me consider access, learner variability, and different ways of engaging with content. Gagné and ADDIE are useful when planning structured sequences, while Action Mapping helps keep the focus on what learners need to do rather than just what content needs to be covered.

I also think SAMR is useful when working with technology, because it encourages me to ask whether a digital tool is genuinely improving the learning task or simply replacing a paper version of the same thing.

I do not want my work to feel jargon-heavy, but I do want it to be informed. The frameworks sit underneath the design. The learner should experience clarity, structure, and purpose rather than a list of theories.

### How do you evaluate whether a learning experience is successful?

I evaluate a learning experience by asking whether it helped learners move toward the intended outcome in a way that was clear, manageable, and meaningful.

Completion data is useful, but it is not enough. I look at the quality of responses, the level of independence, the kinds of questions learners ask, how well they can explain their thinking, and whether the evidence they produce actually aligns with the intended learning.

I also pay attention to friction. If learners are getting stuck in the same place, that usually tells me something about the design. The instruction might be unclear, the task might be too large, the example might be missing, or the assessment expectation might not be visible enough.

A successful learning experience should work for the learner and for the person facilitating it. If the module looks polished but needs constant rescue, it is not finished.

## Technology & AI

### How do you approach AI in education?

I see AI as a literacy rather than a tool. Just as students learned to search the web effectively, they now need to learn how to collaborate with AI systems, evaluate outputs critically, and understand where human judgement remains essential.

In education, I think the question is less “Should students use AI?” and more “How do we teach students to use it responsibly, transparently, and thoughtfully?” That means making the process visible. Students need to understand prompting, iteration, evaluation, bias, voice, and the difference between assistance and substitution.

In a learning design context, I am interested in AI because it changes both the work learners will enter and the way learning experiences can be created. It can support drafting, reflection, feedback, ideation, and personalised support, but it also requires stronger judgement from the learner.

The aim is not to outsource thinking. The aim is to help learners think with better tools.

### What tools do you use most often?

The tools most central to my practice are probably the simplest ones: markdown and spreadsheets.

Markdown helps me structure thinking clearly. It is portable, clean, and easy to adapt into web content, documentation, learning materials, and project notes. Spreadsheets help me build systems. I use them for planning, tracking, assessment logic, progress visibility, moderation support, and data-informed decision-making.

Beyond that, I use the tools that suit the task. Google Workspace, Microsoft Teams, LMS platforms, Articulate, Figma, Adobe tools, GitHub, VS Code, HTML, CSS, Bootstrap, JavaScript, Python, and AI tools all have a place, but I try not to begin with the platform.

My preference is open first, closed when needed. I like flexible systems that can be understood, adapted, and maintained without unnecessary complexity.

### How do you balance innovation with pedagogy?

I balance innovation with pedagogy by making the learning purpose the starting point. New tools are interesting, but they are only valuable if they improve the learning experience.

Before using a tool, I ask what it changes for the learner. Does it make the task clearer? Does it improve access? Does it support feedback? Does it allow a more authentic form of evidence? Does it help learners practise something meaningful?

That is why I am comfortable experimenting with AI, web tools, spreadsheets, LMS workflows, and interactive modules, but I am also cautious about novelty. A technically impressive resource is not automatically a strong learning experience.

Innovation should serve the design. The learner should not have to fight the tool to reach the learning.

### What opportunities and risks do you see in AI?

The opportunity is that AI can support learners through drafting, questioning, summarising, practising, reflecting, and exploring possibilities. It can also help educators and learning designers develop resources, adapt materials, and prototype learning experiences more efficiently.

The risk is that AI can make weak thinking look polished. A learner can produce fluent work without understanding it. That means educators need to pay more attention to process, judgement, and evidence of thinking.

I am also conscious of issues around privacy, bias, academic integrity, equity, and over-reliance. Those concerns are real, but I do not think avoiding AI is a realistic long-term response. Learners need structured opportunities to understand it.

For me, the key is transparency. Show the process. Discuss the choices. Evaluate the output. Keep the human judgement visible.

### Why are you learning Python and web development?

Partly because I enjoy understanding how things work. That has always been part of how I think. I like systems, structure, tools, and the mechanics underneath the surface.

Professionally, Python and web development sit very comfortably alongside my learning design goals. They help me prototype resources, automate repetitive work, understand data, build interactive learning experiences, and communicate more effectively with technical teams.

I do not see coding as a completely separate career direction. I see it as an extension of the same interest that sits behind curriculum and learning design: how do you build systems that help people do something better?

It is both a personal passion and a practical professional skillset.

## Leadership & Collaboration

### How do you support colleagues through change?

I support colleagues through change by making the change feel useful, manageable, and connected to their actual work.

In education, people are often asked to adopt new systems without enough time, clarity, or support. My approach is to reduce that friction. I try to model the workflow, provide examples, explain the purpose, and create structures that make the change easier to understand.

My leadership has been formative across different areas, but the work I feel strongest about is curriculum. When a curriculum structure is clear, when the assessment pathway makes sense, and when the resources are usable, it becomes easier for a team to work together.

I think change is more likely to last when it improves the work rather than simply adding another layer to it.

### What is your approach to professional learning?

I approach professional learning as something that should feed directly back into practice. I am not especially interested in collecting credentials for their own sake. I want to learn things that change what I can build, explain, design, or improve.

That has included formal learning, professional development, certifications, curriculum training, technical courses, and a lot of self-directed experimentation. I tend to learn best when I can connect an idea to a real project.

That is one reason I am building this portfolio publicly. It gives my learning a practical output. If I am learning web development, Python, technical writing, or learning design, I want that learning to become visible in the systems, resources, and case studies I create.

Professional learning should not just sit in a list. It should change the work.

### How do you work with stakeholders?

I work with stakeholders by trying to understand what each group needs from the learning experience.

Students need clarity, relevance, and support. Teachers need resources that are realistic to deliver. Leaders need alignment, evidence, and confidence that the curriculum is being met. Families and community partners need communication that is clear and respectful. Employers or external audiences need to understand the capability being demonstrated.

A lot of my work is translation. I take curriculum requirements, assessment expectations, learning needs, platform constraints, and practical realities, then turn them into something people can use.

That is also why I value documentation. Clear documents, structured resources, and well-designed tracking systems reduce ambiguity and make collaboration easier.

### What leadership experience do you bring?

The leadership experience I would emphasise most is curriculum leadership.

I have developed curriculum structures, assessment tools, learning sequences, and evidence systems across VCE VM, applied learning, Digital Technologies, Literacy, Work Related Skills, and Numeracy contexts. I have also contributed to audit and compliance work, eLearning implementation, school improvement processes, and mentoring, but curriculum is where I feel I do my best work.

My leadership style is practical and systems-focused. I like building structures that help people understand the work, deliver it more confidently, and see how the pieces connect.

For me, leadership is not only about holding a formal title. It is also about improving the quality of the system people are working within.


### How do you build trust within a team?

I build trust by being clear, useful, and reliable.

In a team, people need to know that you understand the work, that you will follow through, and that you are not making things harder than they need to be. I try to communicate clearly, document decisions, share resources, and respect the expertise of the people around me.

I also think trust grows when people feel that their context has been understood. A good idea can still fail if it ignores workload, timing, confidence, or the culture of the team.

My aim is to contribute in ways that make the work more coherent. Good collaboration should leave people feeling clearer, not more confused.
