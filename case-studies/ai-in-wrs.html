Embedding AI Literacy into Work Related Skills

Case study exploring the scaffolded integration of Artificial Intelligence discussion, critical thinking, and ethical reflection into the Work Related Skills curriculum.

Challenge

The rapid adoption of generative AI tools such as ChatGPT created a new challenge within applied learning programs. Students were increasingly using AI to generate responses, complete assessment tasks, and refine written work. While these tools offered genuine opportunities for productivity and support, they also raised important questions about learning, authorship, and assessment.

In many cases, students were copying and pasting AI-generated responses with minimal engagement in the thinking processes required to develop understanding. This created a disconnect between task completion and learning. Traditional responses, such as restricting AI use or reverting to paper-based assessment, were either difficult to implement consistently or failed to acknowledge the growing role that AI will play in future workplaces.

The challenge was not simply how to prevent AI use, but how to redesign learning experiences so that AI became a tool for reflection, critical thinking, and skill development rather than a substitute for them.

Within the Work Related Skills curriculum, this presented an opportunity to address two related needs. First, students required a deeper understanding of how emerging technologies may influence industries, employment pathways, and transferable skills. Second, they needed practical experience using AI responsibly while maintaining ownership of their ideas, decisions, and written communication.

The project therefore sought to reframe AI from a shortcut to task completion into a structured learning partner that could support inquiry, feedback, revision, and metacognitive reflection.

PROJECT PRINCIPLE

The goal was not to determine whether students used AI, but to make their use of AI visible, intentional, and open to discussion.

Design Principles

Critical Thinking

Students were required to evaluate, question, and refine AI-generated responses rather than accept them uncritically. AI became a starting point for thinking rather than a replacement for it.

Human Agency

Learners maintained ownership of decisions, ideas, and final outputs. AI supported the process, but students remained responsible for judgement, reflection, and authorship.

Authentic Assessment

Assessment was redesigned to capture evidence of learning processes, including prompting, revision, reflection, and decision-making, rather than evaluating only finished products.

The project was guided by a set of learning design principles intended to balance the opportunities presented by AI with the cognitive processes required for meaningful learning.

Learning Before Efficiency

While AI can generate responses quickly, rapid completion does not necessarily lead to understanding. Learning activities were therefore designed to ensure students remained active participants in the thinking process rather than passive consumers of generated content.

Authentic Assessment

Rather than assessing only the final product, assessment tasks were redesigned to capture evidence of process. Students were required to demonstrate how AI had been used, document their interactions, and justify decisions made throughout the development of their work.

Structured Cognitive Support

Prompt frameworks and scaffolded activities were introduced to reduce unnecessary cognitive load while maintaining productive challenge. AI was positioned as a support tool that could assist students in organising ideas, refining language, and exploring alternatives without removing the need for critical judgement.

Reflection and Iteration

Students engaged in cycles of drafting, evaluation, revision, and reflection. This approach encouraged retrieval, refinement of ideas, and greater awareness of how their thinking evolved through interaction with AI-generated feedback.

Experiential AI Literacy

Rather than treating AI as a theoretical topic, students worked directly with the technology in authentic learning contexts. This provided opportunities to discuss ethical use, bias, authorship, workplace applications, and the evolving relationship between human expertise and machine-generated content.

Learning Solution

Rather than treating AI as a standalone topic, I designed a two-stage intervention embedded within the Work Related Skills curriculum. The intention was to address both immediate learning needs and longer-term workforce readiness while creating opportunities for students to engage critically and responsibly with emerging technologies.

The solution was implemented at two key points within the program. A Year 10 module was designed to introduce students to the broader implications of AI in workplaces and society, encouraging discussion around transferable skills, automation, and the changing nature of work. A Year 12 module shifted the focus from discussion to application, providing structured opportunities for students to use AI as part of an authentic portfolio development process.

Together, the two modules moved students from understanding AI as a technological phenomenon to using it as a practical learning and workplace tool. Throughout both modules, the emphasis remained on critical thinking, reflection, and human decision-making rather than the generation of content alone.

The design sought to reposition AI from a shortcut to task completion into a structured partner for learning, feedback, drafting, and professional communication.

Year 10 Module: AI Literacy and Future Skills

The Year 10 module was positioned at a key transition point for learners entering both the school and the applied learning pathway. For many students, this represented a shift away from traditional classroom models towards a more authentic, workplace-oriented approach to learning. Introducing AI literacy at this stage provided an opportunity to establish productive habits and expectations before patterns of technology use became entrenched.

The module was designed around a local industry excursion and explored the relationship between emerging technologies, workforce change, and transferable skills. Students investigated how automation and AI may influence different industries while considering the ongoing value of human capabilities such as communication, adaptability, collaboration, and critical thinking.

To reduce barriers to participation, learning activities were delivered through a form-based platform that could be accessed directly from student devices without requiring navigation through a Learning Management System. This design decision reduced friction, increased accessibility, and allowed students to focus on the learning task itself.

Activities combined discussion, reflection, worksheet submissions, and guided inquiry. Rather than presenting AI as either a threat or a solution, the module encouraged students to critically examine its strengths, limitations, and potential impact on future employment pathways. Reflection activities were embedded throughout the learning sequence to help students connect emerging technologies with their own skills, aspirations, and career development.

Year 12 Module: AI-Assisted Portfolio Development

While the Year 10 module focused on developing awareness and critical understanding, the Year 12 module shifted towards practical application. Positioned within Unit 4 of the Work Related Skills curriculum, the project provided students with opportunities to apply AI tools within an authentic professional communication context.

Students were tasked with developing a personal portfolio website that brought together existing learning, workplace experiences, reflections, and newly created content. The website served as both a practical assessment task and a professional artefact that could support future employment, training, or further education pathways.

Throughout the project, students used AI as a structured writing and feedback partner rather than a content generator. Prompt frameworks were provided to support brainstorming, drafting, editing, and refinement activities. Students were encouraged to compare AI-generated suggestions against their own intentions, adapt language to reflect their personal voice, and make conscious decisions about which recommendations to accept, reject, or modify.

A key design principle was maintaining visibility of the learning process. Students submitted not only their completed portfolio website, but also records of their AI interactions, reflections on how the technology influenced their work, and written justifications for significant decisions made throughout the development process.

The project culminated in an in-person presentation where students discussed both their portfolio and the process used to create it. This provided an additional layer of authenticity, requiring students to articulate their thinking, explain how AI had been used, and demonstrate ownership of the final product. The combination of digital artefacts, process documentation, reflection, and presentation created multiple forms of evidence that extended beyond the finished website itself.

The final stage of the module introduced students to strategies for using large language models as tools for ongoing learning beyond formal schooling. This connected directly to the curriculum's focus on future pathways while encouraging students to view AI not simply as a productivity tool, but as a resource for self-directed learning, professional growth, and lifelong skill development.

Assessment Design

A key challenge of integrating AI into assessment is ensuring that evidence of learning remains visible. Traditional assessment models often focus on the final product, making it difficult to determine how ideas were developed or what role AI played in the process.

To address this challenge, assessment was designed to capture multiple forms of evidence. Rather than evaluating only the completed portfolio website, students were required to demonstrate the thinking, decision-making, reflection, and communication processes that contributed to its creation.

This approach shifted the focus from product-based assessment towards evidence of learning and authorship.

Why This Matters

The inclusion of AI interaction histories, written reflections, and an in-person presentation created opportunities for students to demonstrate ownership of their work. Students were required not only to produce a professional artefact, but also to explain how it was developed, justify significant decisions, and articulate the role that AI played throughout the process.

By collecting evidence from multiple sources, the assessment design encouraged responsible AI use while maintaining a strong focus on critical thinking, communication, and learner agency.

Reflection

This project reinforced the importance of treating AI as a learning design challenge rather than a technology challenge. Students were already using AI extensively, often viewing it as a tool for producing answers rather than supporting learning. Early discussions revealed common misconceptions about the role of AI in education, alongside limited awareness of how emerging technologies may influence future workplaces and career pathways.

One of the most significant insights was the need for explicit instruction in AI use. Simply providing access to AI tools did not automatically result in productive learning behaviours. Students benefited from structured guidance around prompting, evaluating outputs, identifying bias, and making informed decisions about how AI could support their work.

The most effective design decision was making AI use visible throughout the learning process. By capturing prompt histories, reflections, and decision-making, students were encouraged to engage more thoughtfully with the technology and maintain ownership of their work. This shifted conversations away from whether AI was being used and towards how it was being used.

The project also highlighted the growing importance of AI literacy as a foundational capability. Alongside communication, collaboration, and critical thinking, the ability to work effectively with AI is becoming an increasingly important skill for lifelong learning and future employment.

Perhaps most importantly, the project demonstrated that assessment design remains central to maintaining authentic learning experiences. Rather than attempting to eliminate AI from the learning environment, redesigning assessment to make learning processes visible created opportunities for richer conversations about thinking, authorship, and learner agency.

Future Iterations

The current implementation represents an initial framework for integrating AI literacy within applied learning programs. Future development will focus on expanding both the scope of resources available to teachers and the range of curriculum areas in which the model can be applied.

One priority is the creation of supporting teacher resources, including implementation guides, prompt libraries, assessment exemplars, and professional learning materials. These resources would help build confidence among educators and provide practical strategies for integrating AI into existing learning programs.

The assessment architecture developed through this project also has potential applications beyond Work Related Skills. The underlying principles of process visibility, learner reflection, and authentic evidence collection could be adapted across English, Humanities, Careers, VET, and other applied learning contexts. Future iterations will explore how the framework can be scaled and modified to support different subject areas and learner needs.

Longer term, the project aims to contribute to a broader shift in school culture surrounding AI. Rather than viewing AI primarily through the lens of academic integrity or compliance, the goal is to support informed, ethical, and purposeful use of emerging technologies. By helping students develop AI literacy and supporting teachers to integrate AI confidently into their practice, the framework seeks to position AI as a tool for critical thinking, professional growth, and lifelong learning.

As AI technologies continue to evolve, learning programs will need to evolve alongside them. Future iterations of this project will continue to explore how learning design, assessment, and emerging technologies can work together to create meaningful and authentic learning experiences.

KEY TAKEAWAY

AI integration is not primarily a technology problem. It is a learning design challenge that requires thoughtful assessment, visible thinking, and deliberate opportunities for learner reflection.
